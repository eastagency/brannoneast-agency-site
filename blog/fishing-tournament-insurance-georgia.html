Fishing Tournament Insurance in Georgia — What Every Organizer and Angler Needs to Know

By Brannon East · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read
← Back to Blog

If you run a bass tournament in Georgia — or fish in one — there’s a good chance nobody has talked to you about insurance. That’s a problem. One accident on the water, one injured angler, one spectator lawsuit, and an uninsured tournament can cost far more than any first-place payout.

At The East Agency, we now offer fishing tournament insurance through one of the most respected specialty carriers in the country — A++ rated by A.M. Best and backed by the Berkshire Hathaway family. Here’s what you need to know.

Georgia’s Fishing Tournament Scene

Georgia is one of the best bass fishing states in the Southeast, and the tournament trail is active year-round. Lake Allatoona alone hosts dozens of competitive events annually, with two of the most-used launch points being Blockhouse Boat Ramp (164 Sandtown Rd SE, Cartersville) and Gatewood Boat Ramp — both accessible and popular for competitive anglers across Bartow County and beyond.

But the Georgia tournament scene extends far beyond Allatoona. Major fisheries that regularly host tournaments include:

  • Lake Allatoona — Cartersville / Bartow County / Cherokee County
  • Lake Lanier — Hall County / Forsyth County
  • Carters Lake — Murray County
  • West Point Lake — Troup / Heard Counties
  • Lake Oconee & Lake Sinclair — Central Georgia
  • Lake Hartwell — Northeast Georgia / South Carolina border
  • Clarks Hill / Thurmond Lake — East Georgia
  • Chattahoochee River corridor — Metro Atlanta

From the Allatoona Team Trail and Four Seasons Team Tournament Trail to the IBEW 613 Bass Tournament in Cartersville, to charity tournaments at marinas across the state — Georgia anglers are competing constantly. Most of that competition is uninsured.

Why Tournament Organizers Need Insurance

Here’s the reality: as a tournament director, you are personally exposed to liability the moment anglers launch from a ramp you organized. The venue didn’t insure your event. The Army Corps of Engineers didn’t insure your event. The participants’ boat insurance covers their vessel — not your liability as the organizer.

Incidents that create real claims include:

  • A participant slips and falls on the dock during check-in
  • Two boats collide at the ramp during a morning blast-off
  • A spectator is injured at the weigh-in
  • A participant’s boat damages marina property
  • An alcohol-related incident at an awards ceremony

Any one of these can result in a lawsuit naming the tournament organizer, the club, or the title sponsor. Without a policy, you’re personally on the hook.

Many Georgia venues — including Army Corps-managed ramps on Lake Allatoona — require a certificate of insurance before issuing an event permit. We can typically have a certificate ready within 24 hours of binding coverage.

What Fishing Tournament Insurance Covers

Through our specialty carrier, The East Agency can place policies covering:

  • General Liability — Your primary protection as an organizer. Covers bodily injury and property damage claims arising from tournament activities. Limits available up to $1 million per occurrence / $3 million aggregate, with higher limits available for larger events.
  • Participant Liability — Extends coverage to registered anglers for incidents that occur during competition on the water.
  • Sponsor Protection — Corporate and title sponsors increasingly require additional insured status before signing onto an event. We can structure the policy to include your sponsors, which often makes the difference in landing a major title sponsor.
  • Host Liquor Liability — If your weigh-in, awards ceremony, or any part of the event involves alcohol service, this coverage protects the organizer from liability for alcohol-related incidents.
  • Event Cancellation — Protects entry fee revenue and pre-paid expenses if the tournament must be cancelled or postponed due to severe weather, venue closure, or other covered perils. Critical for multi-day events with high entry fees.

Who Should Be Looking at This Coverage

  • Bass club presidents running team trails and open tournaments
  • Charities using a fishing tournament as their primary fundraiser
  • Tournament directors running pro-am or open events on any Georgia lake
  • Corporate event planners organizing fishing outings for clients or employees
  • Boat ramp and marina operators hosting events on their property
  • High school and youth fishing programs running competitive events

How Much Does It Cost

For a single-day tournament with under 100 participants, a general liability policy typically runs a few hundred dollars — often less than the cost of a first-place trophy. Multi-day events, larger participant counts, and events with alcohol service will cost more, but coverage scales accordingly.
