Insurance, Explained.

Plain-English definitions for the terms your policy is full of — but nobody ever explained to you.

The Basics

Terms You'll See on Every Policy

Core

Deductible

The amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance kicks in. A higher deductible lowers your premium — a lower deductible means less surprise at claim time.

Example: $1,000 deductible on auto — you cover the first $1,000 in repairs, your insurer covers the rest.

Core

Premium

Your payment — monthly, quarterly, or annually — to keep your policy active. Think of it as the cost of having your insurer ready to step in when life goes sideways.

Core

Policy Limit

The maximum your insurer will pay for a covered loss. If damages exceed your limit, you're responsible for the difference. Choosing the right limits matters more than most people realize.

Core

Exclusion

Something your policy specifically does NOT cover. Reading exclusions is as important as reading what's covered. Common examples: flood damage on a homeowners policy, intentional acts, normal wear and tear.

Core

Declarations Page

The one-page summary at the front of your policy — who's covered, what's covered, your limits, deductibles, premium, and policy dates. If you only read one page of your policy, read this one.

Also called the "dec page" by agents and carriers.

Core

Endorsement / Rider

An add-on that modifies your base policy — adding coverage, removing it, or changing a condition. Jewelry coverage, home business coverage, scheduled equipment — all endorsements.

Core

Underwriting

The process an insurer uses to evaluate your risk and decide whether to offer coverage — and at what price. They look at your history, your property, your location, and more.

Core

Grace Period

Extra time after your due date to pay before your policy cancels. Most policies allow 10–30 days. Don't rely on it — a lapse in coverage, even for one day, can cost you more than you saved.

Core

Binder

Temporary proof of insurance issued before your official policy documents arrive. Common at car dealerships or real estate closings when you need proof of coverage immediately.

Core

Subrogation

After your insurer pays your claim, they have the right to pursue the party responsible for your loss to recover what they paid. You won't see it happen — but it protects your rates long-term.

Auto Insurance

What's Actually in Your Auto Policy

Auto

Liability Coverage

Covers injuries and property damage you cause to other people. Required by law in most states. It does NOT cover your own vehicle or your own injuries — only what you do to others.

Written as limits: 100/300/100 means $100K per person / $300K per accident / $100K property damage.

Auto

Comprehensive Coverage

Covers damage to your vehicle from events other than a collision — theft, hail, fire, flooding, hitting a deer, a tree falling on it. If it wasn't a crash with another vehicle, it's probably comprehensive.

Auto

Collision Coverage

Covers damage to your vehicle when you're in an accident — regardless of fault. Hit a guardrail, another car, or a bad pothole? Collision pays for repairs after your deductible.

Auto

Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist

Protects you when the driver who hit you has no insurance — or not enough. In Georgia, roughly 1 in 8 drivers is uninsured. This coverage is more important than most people think.

Written as UM/UIM on your policy documents.

Auto

GAP Insurance

If your car is totaled or stolen, your insurer pays what the car is worth today — not what you owe on the loan. GAP covers the difference. Critical if you financed with little down payment.

Auto

SR-22

Not insurance itself — a certificate your insurer files with the state proving you carry required liability coverage. Usually required after a DUI, reckless driving, or driving uninsured. It raises your premium.

Home & Property

What Protects Your Home

Property

Actual Cash Value (ACV)

What your property is worth TODAY — after depreciation. If your 10-year-old roof is destroyed, you get what a 10-year-old roof is worth, not what a new one costs. Lower premium, smaller payout at claim time.

Property

Replacement Cost Value (RCV)

What it costs to replace damaged property with NEW materials at today's prices — no depreciation deducted. Higher premium, but you actually get made whole when something goes wrong. Almost always worth it.

Property

Named Perils

Your policy only covers events that are specifically listed — fire, windstorm, theft, lightning, etc. If the cause of your damage isn't on the list, you're not covered. Most basic home policies work this way.

Property

Open Perils / All-Risk

Covers ALL causes of loss EXCEPT those specifically excluded. Broader protection than named perils. If your policy doesn't say it's excluded, you're covered. Worth asking your agent about.

Property

Loss of Use

If your home is uninhabitable after a covered loss, this pays for your hotel, rental, and extra living expenses while repairs are made. Often overlooked — and absolutely critical when you need it.

Property

Flood Insurance

Standard homeowners policies do NOT cover flooding. It must be purchased separately — through the NFIP or a private insurer. If you're anywhere near water, don't skip this conversation.

Property

Umbrella Policy

Extra liability that kicks in above your home and auto limits — typically in $1 million increments. Covers serious accidents, lawsuits, and situations where your underlying policy falls short. One of the best values in insurance.

Property

Dwelling vs. Personal Property

Dwelling coverage protects the structure of your home. Personal property coverage protects your belongings inside it — furniture, electronics, clothing. These are separate limits on your policy.

Life & Health

Protecting the People Who Depend on You

Life

Term Life Insurance

Coverage for a set period — 10, 20, or 30 years. If you die during the term, your beneficiary gets the death benefit. No cash value, no investment component. Pure, affordable income replacement. Best for most families.

Life

Whole Life Insurance

Permanent coverage with a cash value component that grows tax-deferred over time. More expensive than term, but it never expires and builds value you can borrow against. Best suited for specific estate planning needs.

Life

Death Benefit

The amount paid to your beneficiary when you die. Make sure it's enough to replace your income, pay off the mortgage, and cover your family's future needs. This is the number that actually matters.

Life

Beneficiary

The person — or people — who receive your death benefit. Name primary and contingent (backup) beneficiaries, and keep them updated after major life events: marriage, divorce, having kids.

Health

Out-of-Pocket Maximum

The most you'll pay in a calendar year for covered services. Once you hit this number, your insurance covers 100% of in-network costs for the rest of the year. Know yours before you ever need it.

Health

Copay vs. Coinsurance

A copay is a fixed amount per visit ($30 for a doctor visit). Coinsurance is a percentage you pay after your deductible — you pay 20%, insurer covers 80%. Most plans use both, depending on the service.

Business Insurance

Protecting What You've Built

Business

BOP (Business Owner's Policy)

Bundles general liability and commercial property into one package — usually at a better rate than buying them separately. Built for small to mid-size businesses. A great starting point for most business owners.

Business

General Liability

Covers bodily injury or property damage your business causes to others. If a customer slips in your store or your crew damages a client's property — general liability responds. Every business needs this.

Business

E&O (Errors & Omissions)

Also called Professional Liability. Covers claims that your services caused a client financial harm — due to mistakes, bad advice, or failure to deliver. Essential for consultants, agents, contractors, and service providers.

Business

Workers' Compensation

Covers employees injured on the job — medical costs, lost wages, and rehabilitation. Required by law in Georgia for most businesses with employees. Protects your workers and shields your business from lawsuits.

Business

Commercial Auto

Your personal auto policy does NOT cover a vehicle used for business. Commercial auto covers business-owned vehicles, or personal vehicles regularly used for work. If you drive for work, this conversation needs to happen.
